This is a standalone strategic document that defines the primary and secondary audiences for Connexted, establishes the relationship between them, and creates a measurement framework for evaluating how well the platform serves each audience. This is a documentation task only. No code changes. No modifications to existing files.
This document is built on one foundational principle: Connexted is not in the content game. It is a platform for doers — professionals who are actively working toward something and who need to find other people working toward the same things. Every audience definition, every relationship description, and every measurement in this document must be consistent with that principle. If a definition or metric could belong to a content platform it is the wrong definition or metric.
Section 1 — The primary audience
Define the three primary audience profiles. These are the doers the platform was built for. Write each profile as a concrete specific person in a real situation — not a demographic description but a human being with a specific problem that the platform was designed to solve.
For each profile cover four things. Who they are and what they are actively doing right now. What their doing looks like today and why it is invisible, fragmented, or unsupported. The specific moment that makes them realize their current tools are not designed for them. What they need that no existing platform provides.
Profile 1 — The professional in transition. Someone who left or is leaving an organizational role and is actively building something new. They are doing real work — contracting, learning, developing methodology, building a portfolio, finding collaborators — but every platform they use was designed to display what they were not what they are becoming. Their doing is invisible. Their LinkedIn shows their last title. Their personal website, if they have one, is static. Nothing shows the active professional they are right now.
Profile 2 — The solopreneur or independent practitioner. Someone working alone or in a very small team — coaching, consulting, researching, advising, creating. Their doing is scattered across platforms they do not own, formatted for algorithms that do not understand what they do, and disconnected from the community that would most benefit from it. Their best work — the methodologies, the AI tools, the frameworks they have spent years developing — is invisible to everyone except their current clients.
Profile 3 — The startup founding team. Two to four people building something together with no IT infrastructure, no enterprise tools budget, and no platform designed for the way they actually work. Their doing — the pitching, the building, the validating, the iterating — is either invisible to the people they need to impress or scattered across tools that do not connect to each other or to a professional presence that compounds over time.
Section 2 — The secondary audience
Define the five secondary audience profiles. These are the people who create the conditions that make the primary audience's experience valuable. Without them the platform has nothing for the primary audience to find, join, walk toward, or be found through. But they are secondary not because they are less important — they are equally important — but because the primary audience can begin to get value from the platform before the secondary audience arrives in numbers.
For each profile cover three things. What they bring to the platform that the primary audience cannot create for itself. What the platform gives them that they cannot get from any existing tool. One concrete example of what their presence on the platform makes possible for a primary audience member.
Profile 1 — The event organizer. Brings gatherings of doers and gives those gatherings a digital home that persists after the event ends.
Profile 2 — The mentor. Walks a road the mentee is trying to walk. Creates Pathways that give the primary audience a structured arc from where they are to where they are going.
Profile 3 — The incubator or accelerator leader. Brings cohorts of doers to the platform, credentials their progress, and gives their doing institutional weight that compounds over time.
Profile 4 — The instructor or program host. Creates structured learning experiences that doers need to develop the capability their doing requires.
Profile 5 — The marketing or business development professional. Makes their company findable by the right doers at the right stage through the Markets function — not through advertising but through demonstrated participation in the doing of the community.
Section 3 — The relationship between primary and secondary
Write three paragraphs explaining how primary and secondary audiences depend on each other and how the network effect emerges from that interdependence.
The first paragraph explains what the primary audience can do without the secondary audience present. A doer can create a profile, document their work in activity containers, join topic-based circles, and begin to be found through the discovery engine from day one. The free tier supports this entirely. The platform delivers value to an individual doer before any secondary audience member arrives.
The second paragraph explains what becomes possible when secondary audience members arrive and at what ratios. One mentor with four active mentees in a specific topic creates a visible credentialed community that attracts more doers with the same intention. One event organizer with thirty attendees creates thirty primary audience members with a shared context who already know each other. One incubator with fifteen startups brings thirty to sixty highly active doers simultaneously — more primary audience engagement from one organizational relationship than from sixty individual acquisition conversations.
The third paragraph explains the compounding effect. A primary audience member who finds a mentor through the platform, walks a Pathway, earns a badge, and has their doing documented and credentialed becomes a more valuable member of the community — their presence attracts other doers and validates the secondary audience member's investment in the platform. Value compounds in both directions simultaneously.
Section 4 — What the platform is not for
One paragraph each for three types of people the platform was not designed to serve and why. This section is not a judgment about those people — it is a clarity statement about where the platform's design choices create friction rather than flow.
The passive consumer. Someone who wants to find and consume content without participating in any doing. The platform was not designed for passive consumption. Every feature assumes active participation — joining, contributing, completing, walking, building. A passive consumer will find the platform confusing because it keeps asking them what they are trying to do and they do not have an answer.
The broadcaster. Someone whose primary goal is to reach the largest possible audience for their content. The platform was not designed for reach maximization. It was designed for relevance — finding the right people not the most people. A broadcaster optimizing for views, shares, and follower counts will find the platform limiting because it routes engagement through shared intention not through algorithmic amplification.
The enterprise team. A large organization with an IT department, a procurement process, and a requirement for enterprise-grade administration, compliance, and integration. The platform was not designed for enterprise scale or enterprise complexity. Its value comes from the lightness and flexibility that makes it accessible to doers who work independently or in small teams. An enterprise team will find it under-engineered for their requirements by design.
Section 5 — The measurement framework
This section creates the metrics that determine whether the platform is working for each audience. These metrics must be grounded in doing not in content consumption. Every metric that could belong to a content platform is the wrong metric.
Write the measurement framework in three parts.
Part one — individual doer metrics. The metrics that show whether a primary audience member is getting value from the platform. Cover six metrics with a one-sentence description of what each one measures and why it matters:
Intention clarity score — does the member have a complete topic profile, a looking-for status, and a headline that reflects what they are working toward. Measures whether the platform knows what the member is trying to do.
Doing depth — how many active containers does the member have in progress or completed. A member with zero containers is present on the platform but not doing on it. A member with three or more active containers is a doer.
Discovery rate — how often is the member found by other members through topic search, similar member matching, or circle discovery rather than through direct invitation. Measures whether the platform is making their doing visible to the right people.
Connection quality — how many connections did the member make through shared activity rather than through a follow or a connection request. Measures whether the platform is creating the kind of relationship it promises.
Pathway progress — if the member is following a Pathway how far along are they and how recently did they complete an activity. Measures whether the platform is delivering on its promise of organized progression.
Return engagement — how often does the member come back to do something rather than just to check. Measures whether the platform is a place they work not just a place they visit.
Part two — community health metrics. The metrics that show whether the platform as a whole is building the right kind of community. Cover four metrics:
Doing to browsing ratio — what percentage of active sessions result in a container activity, a circle contribution, a Pathway step, or an event attendance versus passive browsing. A healthy platform has more doing than browsing.
Primary to secondary ratio — for every organizer, mentor, instructor, or incubator on the platform how many active primary audience members are they serving. The target ratio is one secondary audience member for every fifteen to twenty active primary audience members. Below that the supply side is undersupplied. Above it the demand side is underserved.
Cross-topic discovery rate — how often do members from different topic areas find and connect with each other through the platform's matching engine. Measures whether the network effect is working across the full community not just within topic silos.
Organizational cohort activation — what percentage of members brought to the platform through an organizational relationship — an incubator cohort, a mentor's mentees, an event organizer's community — become active doers within thirty days. Measures whether secondary audience members are successfully activating primary audience members.
Part three — platform health metrics. The metrics that show whether the platform is healthy as a business. Cover three metrics:
Intention to doing conversion — what percentage of members who create a profile and express an intention go on to create at least one active container within their first thirty days. This is the most important single metric for platform health. A member who expressed an intention but never did anything on the platform did not get value.
Pathway completion rate — what percentage of members who begin a Pathway complete it. Measures whether the platform's core credentialing promise is being fulfilled.
Organizational retention — what percentage of organizational relationships — incubators, event organizers, sponsors, mentors — renew their engagement after their first cohort or season. Measures whether the secondary audience finds the platform worth continuing to invest in.
Section 6 — The audience test
End the document with a practical tool — a one-page test that can be used to evaluate any feature, any marketing message, any product decision, or any partnership against the audience definition. Write it as a series of questions that should be asked about any decision before it is made.
Does this serve a doer — someone who is actively working toward something — or does it serve a passive consumer or a broadcaster?
Does this help a doer find other doers working toward the same thing, or does it help someone reach more people with their content?
Does this make doing more visible, more organized, or more connected — or does it make content more distributable?
Does this serve the primary audience directly, or does it support the secondary audience in serving the primary audience better?
Does this add a capability the right audience genuinely needs, or does it add a feature that makes the platform look more complete?
Would the professional in transition, the solopreneur, or the startup founder find this immediately useful for something they are actively working on right now?
If the answer to the first question in each pair is yes the decision is aligned with the audience definition. If the answer to the second question in each pair is yes the decision is drifting toward the content game.
Format and tone
Write in clear direct prose. No bullet point lists within paragraphs. No jargon. Every sentence should be specific enough that it could not describe a different platform. The document should feel like a strategic brief written by someone who has thought carefully about who this platform is for and why that matters for every decision that follows.
