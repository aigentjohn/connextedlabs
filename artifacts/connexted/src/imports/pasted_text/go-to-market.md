Section 1 — The problem
Write this for someone who has never heard of Connexted. Describe the specific situation of the AI-augmented professional in 2025 — the solopreneur, the micro startup team of two to four people, the professional in career transition. Their work is scattered across Ghost, YouTube, Notion, LinkedIn, Gamma, AI-generated sites, and a dozen other tools. Their discoverability is declining as SEO weakens, as algorithmic reach on social platforms shrinks, and as personal AI assistants replace Google search for the people who would otherwise find them. Their collaboration is fragmented across specialized platforms none of which talk to each other. They are creating more than ever and being found less than ever. They have no enterprise IT department, no collaboration budget, and no platform that was built for them rather than for a company.

Section 2 — The ideal customer
Define three specific ideal customer profiles. For each one write a concrete paragraph describing who they are, what their current day looks like, what specific moment of frustration drives them to look for something better, and what they need that nothing currently gives them.
Profile 1 — The solopreneur creator. Someone who coaches, consults, teaches, or creates content independently. They have audiences scattered across platforms they don't own. They have AI tools they've built or curated that make them more effective but have no way to share or get credit for. They want a professional home that aggregates their presence without replacing where they already create.
Profile 2 — The micro startup team. Two to four people building something together with no IT infrastructure, no enterprise tools budget, and no onboarding process for new team members. They collaborate in a mix of chat, shared docs, and verbal agreements. They need structured workspaces for specific activities — pitching investors, tracking sprints, running standups — without the overhead of enterprise tools that assume a 50-person company.
Profile 3 — The professional in transition. Someone between roles, pivoting industries, or building a consulting practice after a corporate career. Their LinkedIn profile shows where they were, not what they're doing now. They are learning, building, and creating but have no platform that shows that journey in a credible, structured way. They need a community that sees them as they are becoming, not as they were.

Section 4 — The activity layer
This section explains the core design philosophy that makes Connexted different from every other platform. Write it clearly for a non-technical reader.
The key idea: Connexted does not care what format your content is in. It cares what you are doing with it. Every container on the platform defines a social contract — an intention — not a file type. A Pitch invites review and feedback. A Sprint commits to time-boxed work. A Build documents work in progress. A Prompt invites a response. A Library curates resources worth returning to. A Standup creates async accountability. Whether the content behind any of these is a slide deck, a website, a YouTube video, an AI-generated document, a GPT link, a Claude Project URL, or anything else that exists on the internet is completely irrelevant to the platform. The URL is the content. The container is the intention.
Explain why this matters: it means the platform is format-agnostic in a world where formats are changing faster than any platform can keep up with. It means no storage liability, no upload friction, no format obsolescence. It means a member can share an AI tool they built, a course they created on another platform, a pitch deck in Gamma, and a blog post on Ghost — and all of it becomes part of their indexed, discoverable presence on Connexted without moving a single file.

Section 5 — The five user stories
Write five concrete user stories. Each one follows the same three-act structure: here is what this person does today and the specific friction it creates, here is the same situation on Connexted, here is what becomes possible that was not possible before. Make each story feel like a real person in a real situation, not a marketing persona.
Story 1 — The Meetup organizer who built a 1,900-member community on a platform that owns all the member data, sends all the communications, and captures all the value. They run great events but they don't own the relationship with a single attendee.
Story 2 — The LinkedIn creator who has 6,000 connections and 90,000 impressions but no way to take those conversations deeper, no way to convert attention into community, and no way to own the relationship with the people who engage with their content.
Story 3 — The solopreneur coach who has built three custom AI tools that make their coaching dramatically more effective — a GPT for session prep, a prompt library for client exercises, a Claude Project for follow-up — but has no way to share these with their community, get credit for building them, or use them as a demonstration of their expertise.
Story 4 — The two-person startup that pitches investors every week using a different deck in a different format sent as an email attachment each time. No version control, no feedback trail, no way to see who actually looked at it, no professional context around the pitch.
Story 5 — The professional in transition who has been doing contract work, taking courses, building an AI-assisted portfolio, and developing real expertise for eight months — but whose LinkedIn still shows their last full-time job title and whose new work is invisible to everyone who might hire or refer them.

Section 6 — Why not the alternatives

The combination is the most important comparison — explain specifically what is lost when a professional tries to stitch these tools together and why the cost is not just money but data ownership, discoverability, and coherence of presence.

Section 7 — The discoverability engine
Explain how topics, tags, and the activity containers work together to create a discoverability surface that no individual tool can replicate. A member who joins Connexted and tags their profile, their circles, and their activity containers with relevant topics becomes findable not just by other members browsing those topics but potentially by AI agents navigating structured professional profiles. Explain the omnichannel angle — a member's Ghost blog, YouTube channel, AI tools, and pitch decks all become part of one indexed presence without moving anything. Explain why URL-first content sharing is the mechanism that makes this possible and why it is a privacy and ownership stance, not a missing feature.

Section 8 — The launch sequence
Describe the three-phase rollout model and why incremental is the right approach.
Phase 1 — the platform launches with one operator running their own courses, programs, and community. The Meetup group and LinkedIn audience convert first. This phase proves the member experience works before adding complexity.
Phase 2 — two to three invited instructors and coaches join as hosts. The multi-host model gets proven with people the operator knows and can support directly. The revenue share model activates.
Phase 3 — the platform opens to applications from instructors and community leaders. The marketplace activates. The discovery engine has enough content and members to create genuine network effects.
Explain what each phase proves and why skipping phases increases risk without increasing speed.
